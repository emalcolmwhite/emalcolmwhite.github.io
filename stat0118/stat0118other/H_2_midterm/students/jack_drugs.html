Introduction

This analysis focuses on national annual pharmaceutical drug spending in Latvia, Mexico, and the United States in the 2000s and early 2010s. While the term drug spending is used throughout the analysis, the data includes only pharmaceutical drugs, not recreational drugs.

Research Question: How does annual drug spending in the United States compare to a country with relatively moderate spending and a country with relatively low spending? How do they compare over time?

The three countries (Latvia - LVA, Mexico - MEX, and the United States - USA) were selected from the larger set of countries in the data because they represented a range of spending behaviors. Generally, the United States was a relatively high-spending nation, Mexico was a relatively moderate-spending nation, and Latvia was a relatively low-spending nation.

The data comes from the Organisation for Economic Cooperation and Development (https://data.oecd.org/healthres/pharmaceutical-spending.htm). The data includes the following variables:

  • Location (displayed in 3-letter country code)
  • Time (displayed in year)
  • pc_healthxp (this is the percent of healthcare funding that went specifically towards pharmaceutical drugs)
  • pc_gdp (this is the percent of the overall national annual gross domestic product that went specifically towards pharmaceutical drugs)
  • usd_cap (this is the average amount (in USD) of money per capita that went towards pharmaceutical drugs)
  • total_spend (this is the total amount (in USD) of money that went towards pharmaceutical drugs)

The analysis will assess and compare spending between the three countries across time through both total spending and percent of annual GDP spent on pharmaceutical drugs.

This figure shows annual drug spending for Latvia, Mexico, and the United States as a percent of national gross domestic product (GDP). Note that while Mexico experienced a very sharp increase around 2002-2003, the United States has contributed a greater percentage of its GDP to drug spending since the late 2000s.

Discussion

The fact that the United States spends a much larger amount of money and a larger percentage of its GDP on drugs than most other nations is significant. While research suggests that there are multiple unique factors that make overall healthcare spending in the United States so high compared to other nations, drug spending is likely high simply because of marked-up drug prices. The United States does not consume significantly more pharmaceutical drugs per capita than near-peer nations.

The rapid increase in percent of Mexican GDP allocated to drug spending during 2003 may be attributed to the brief decrease in national GDP that occurred during that year. However, the national economy recovered quickly in 2004 and 2005, so there may be additional explanations for why the return to normal drug spending occurred so slowly.

Additionally, although the United States spends an extremely high absolute dollar amount on drugs, the percent of national GDP spent on drugs is not much higher than Latvia and Mexico. This may simply be attributable to the fact that the United States has a relatively very high GDP.

One major limitation of this analysis was the time frame that data was available for. In this data set, many countries only had data available beginning in the 2000s (although some had data as early as the 1970s). It also would have been helpful to have more recent data (late 2010s and early 2020s). The additional data would have made trends more pronounced and provided more confidence in any comparative assessments between countries. There was also only data for a seemingly random selection of countries that excluded key near-peer nations such as Russia and China, which would have been helpful for comparison.

An additional area of potential exploration and data visualization, in addition to options available with more data, would be a visualization that included both actual year-to-year GDPs for the selected countries in addition to the percent of GDP put towards drug spending. For example, in Mexico, even though the percent of GDP put towards drug spending has been steadily decreasing, the economy has been growing and annual GDP increasing.
